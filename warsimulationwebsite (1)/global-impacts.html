Cultural, environmental & global impacts

Military simulation ripples far beyond the training centre — shaping culture, the environment, and how nations like Australia cooperate with the wider world.

🎭 Cultural impacts

Military simulation sits at a fascinating crossroads of gaming culture and military culture. Many simulators are built on the same game engines as commercial video games, and studios such as Bohemia Interactive make both. This shared technology has several cultural effects:

  • Blurring games and defence: popular war games draw on real military advice, while the military borrows game technology — each shapes how the public pictures conflict.
  • Representation & realism: the environments, languages and cultures recreated in simulations influence how soldiers understand the regions they may operate in — raising questions about stereotyping and cultural sensitivity.
  • A "gamer-to-soldier" pipeline: familiarity with controllers and 3D worlds means new recruits often adapt to simulators quickly, and recruitment increasingly uses game-like experiences.
  • Global tech culture: simulation has become a respected engineering field, with international conferences (e.g. I/ITSEC) and a worldwide professional community.
// Environment

🌱 Environmental impacts

Simulation is a rare case where "more screens" can mean less pollution.

✓ Greener than live training

  • Replaces real flights, tank manoeuvres and ship voyages — slashing fuel burn and carbon emissions.
  • Fewer live-fire exercises means less ammunition, fewer explosives and less damage to training grounds and wildlife.
  • Reduces wear on expensive equipment, extending its life and cutting manufacturing demand.

✕ A hidden footprint

  • Powerful simulators and the data centres behind them consume significant electricity.
  • Constant hardware upgrades create electronic waste (e-waste) — old headsets, GPUs and motion rigs.
  • Manufacturing high-end computers, GPUs and VR gear uses rare minerals and energy.
Net effect Most analysts agree that, overall, shifting training into simulation reduces the military's environmental footprint compared with equivalent live exercises — provided the electricity comes from increasingly clean sources.

🌏 Local & global impacts

🇦🇺

Local — Australia

Australia is geographically vast and far from its allies, so simulation is especially valuable. The ADF uses simulators to train F-35 pilots, Army units and Navy crews efficiently, and a growing local industry of simulation and defence-technology companies supports skilled jobs. Simulation also lets the ADF rehearse disaster relief — like bushfire and flood responses — not just combat.

🌐

Global — the wider world

Distributed simulation lets allied nations (such as NATO members and Australia's partners) train together across continents in one shared virtual battlespace, improving "interoperability". However, access is unequal: wealthy nations afford cutting-edge systems while poorer countries cannot, widening a global capability gap.

What is military simulation?

Before we judge whether it's good or bad, we need to understand the technology. This page explains what military simulation is, the three "types" of simulation armed forces use, and the engineering that makes a virtual war feel convincing.

A clear definition

Military simulation (sometimes called "war-gaming" or "synthetic training") is the use of software, hardware and modelling to imitate real combat situations. Instead of sending real soldiers, tanks, ships and aircraft into a real exercise — which is expensive, dangerous and can damage the environment — defence forces recreate those situations on computers and in specially built simulators.

The purpose is to let personnel practise skills, rehearse a specific mission, test new equipment and explore strategy in a safe, repeatable environment. A trainee can "crash" a $100-million jet in a simulator, learn from the mistake, and try again five minutes later — something impossible in real life.

Key idea Military simulation is not about playing for fun. It is a serious training and decision-making tool built to the same engineering standards as the real equipment it represents.

How it works: the technology behind a virtual battlefield

A modern military simulator combines several technologies working together in real time:

  • Physics & maths engines — calculate how aircraft, projectiles, vehicles and weather behave according to real-world physics, so the simulation reacts realistically.
  • 3D environments & terrain databases — built from real satellite and mapping data so soldiers can rehearse in a digital copy of an actual location.
  • Motion platforms & hardware mock-ups — hydraulic cockpits, tank turrets and ship bridges that physically tilt and shake to match what's on screen.
  • Head-mounted VR/AR displays — immersive headsets that surround the trainee with the virtual world.
  • Haptic feedback — vibration and force-feedback in controls and even vests, so users "feel" recoil, impacts or terrain.
  • Computer-Generated Forces (CGF) — AI-controlled friendly and enemy units that behave like real troops, so trainees face an unpredictable opponent.
  • Networking — links many simulators together so dozens of people in different rooms (or countries) share the same virtual battle.

The 3 Types of Military Simulation

Defence forces worldwide describe their training as LVC — Live, Virtual & Constructive. INFOGRAPHIC ①

L

LIVE

Real people, real equipment. Actual soldiers using real (often laser-tag style) weapons in field exercises. Highest realism, highest cost & risk.

V

VIRTUAL

Real people, simulated equipment. A pilot flies a full-motion cockpit; a crew operates a virtual tank. Safe, repeatable, immersive.

C

CONSTRUCTIVE

Simulated people & equipment. Commanders move thousands of AI-controlled units on a digital map to test strategy — like a hyper-realistic war-game.

Key features, explained

These are the AI-controlled "enemy" and friendly units. Good CGF use artificial intelligence to take cover, retreat, flank and react to the trainee — meaning no two runs of a scenario are identical. This forces real decision-making instead of memorising a script.
Hydraulic or electric actuators physically move the cockpit or vehicle cab. When the on-screen jet banks left, the whole cabin tilts, tricking the inner ear into believing the motion is real. This is essential for pilots learning to handle disorientation.
Simulators load digital 3D copies of real places built from satellite imagery, GPS and survey data. A unit can rehearse a mission in a digital twin of the exact valley, city or coastline they will later operate in.
Using standards such as DIS and HLA, separate simulators are networked so an aircraft, a tank crew and a command centre — possibly in different countries — all share one synchronised virtual battle. This lets entire joint forces train together.
Headsets immerse the user in the scene, while augmented reality can overlay virtual enemies onto a real training ground. Haptic vests and controls add the sense of touch — recoil, impact and vibration — to deepen the realism.

Systems & platforms that use it

✈️

Full-flight simulators

Certified replicas of cockpits (e.g. F-35, F/A-18, C-130) used to train and re-qualify military pilots.

🪖

VBS (Virtual Battlespace)

A military training game-engine by Bohemia Interactive Simulations, used by armies in over 50 countries.

🖥️

OneSAF / JCATS

Constructive simulations that model thousands of entities for staff and commander war-gaming.

🎖️

US Army STE

The Synthetic Training Environment — a next-gen platform merging live, virtual and constructive training in one system.

🚢

Naval bridge & submarine sims

Recreate ship handling, navigation and combat-information-centre operations for crews.

🏥

Combat medical simulators

Haptic mannequins and VR that let medics rehearse battlefield trauma care under pressure.

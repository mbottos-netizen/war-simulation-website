Positive, negative & social impacts

Military simulation saves lives and money — but it also raises serious concerns. A balanced view weighs both sides, and considers how the technology affects the people who use it.

// Balanced analysis

Benefits vs. drawbacks

✓ Positive impacts

  • Safety: trainees rehearse deadly scenarios — crashes, ambushes, missile attacks — with zero physical risk.
  • Huge cost savings: a simulator hour costs a fraction of flying a real jet or running a live exercise, saving taxpayers millions.
  • Repeatability: a scenario can be replayed instantly to drill a skill until it is mastered.
  • Better decisions: commanders test strategies in war-games and find flaws before lives are at stake.
  • Accessibility: training is available any time, any weather, regardless of equipment availability.
  • Industry & jobs: creates careers in software, engineering, 3D art and data science within the defence sector.

✕ Negative impacts

  • High upfront cost: advanced simulators and the software to run them are extremely expensive to build and maintain.
  • The "reality gap": no simulation perfectly copies real combat — fear, fatigue and chaos are hard to reproduce, risking over-confidence.
  • Over-reliance: forces may train so much virtually that real-world hours and instincts suffer.
  • Possible desensitisation: critics worry that repeatedly "engaging targets" on screen could make violence feel less real.
  • Cyber-security risk: networked simulators and terrain data are valuable targets for hacking and espionage.
  • Health effects: long VR sessions can cause "simulator sickness" — nausea, eye strain and disorientation.

Social impacts — how it affects people & teams

Beyond cost and safety, simulation changes how military personnel work together and behave:

  • Teamwork & communication: networked simulators force crews, squads and command centres to coordinate exactly as they would in real operations, building trust and clear communication.
  • Stress inoculation: by repeatedly facing high-pressure scenarios safely, personnel build resilience and calmer decision-making — a recognised psychological benefit.
  • Inclusion & access: simulation lets people train regardless of physical location or limited equipment, widening who can take part.
  • Mental health considerations: highly realistic combat simulation can be confronting. There is research interest in whether intense exposure affects wellbeing — but the same VR technology is also used to treat PTSD in veterans through controlled exposure therapy.
  • Recruitment & public perception: simulation-style experiences are increasingly used in recruitment, blurring the line between gaming culture and military careers.
Balanced view The same technology that trains people for war is also used to heal them: VR exposure therapy helps veterans manage post-traumatic stress. This shows how a single tool can carry both positive and negative social impacts.

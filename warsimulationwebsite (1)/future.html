New, emerging & future developments

Military simulation is advancing fast. Artificial intelligence, digital twins and immersive AR are reshaping how forces will train in the years ahead — alongside new risks we must watch carefully.

// On the horizon

Emerging technologies

🤖

AI synthetic adversaries

Machine-learning opponents that learn and adapt during a scenario, giving trainees a genuinely unpredictable, intelligent enemy instead of scripted bots.

🧬

Digital twins

Exact virtual copies of real vehicles, ships or even entire battlefields, updated with live data — letting forces test and rehearse on a perfect digital mirror.

🕶️

Augmented-reality live training

AR headsets overlay virtual enemies and effects onto a real training field, blending live and virtual training into one seamless experience.

☁️

Cloud & "military metaverse"

Persistent, cloud-hosted virtual worlds let personnel anywhere log into the same evolving battlespace — a defence version of the metaverse.

🧠

Brain & biometric feedback

Sensors track stress, focus and heart rate to adapt scenario difficulty in real time and measure how trainees cope under pressure.

🦾

Haptic suits & full immersion

Whole-body haptic gear and higher-resolution VR aim to make the virtual battlefield almost indistinguishable from the real thing.

// Evolution

From the Link Trainer to the metaverse

1929

The Link Trainer

Edwin Link builds the first mechanical flight simulator — pilots train safely on the ground for the first time.

1980s–90s

Networked simulation (SIMNET)

The US connects tank and aircraft simulators into one shared virtual battle, inventing distributed simulation.

2000s

Game engines go military

Commercial game technology (e.g. VBS) is adapted into serious training tools used worldwide.

2010s–20s

VR, AR & the Synthetic Training Environment

Affordable headsets and the US Army's STE push immersive, blended LVC training mainstream.

Future

AI-driven, cloud-based synthetic worlds

Persistent, intelligent, always-on virtual battlespaces accessible from anywhere.

✓ Future opportunities

  • Cheaper, safer and far more realistic training for every branch of the military.
  • Faster, smarter strategic planning powered by AI war-gaming.
  • Crossover benefits for civilians — disaster response, aviation, surgery and education.
  • Growing high-tech job market in simulation engineering and AI.

✕ Future risks

  • Over-reliance on AI predictions that may be wrong in real conflict.
  • Cyber-attacks on cloud-based "metaverse" training systems.
  • Ethical worries about ever-more-realistic violence and desensitisation.
  • A widening gap between nations that can and cannot afford the technology.
// Final reflection

Conclusion

Military simulation has quietly become one of the most important technologies in modern defence. What began in 1929 as a wooden box that pitched and rolled has grown into vast networked worlds where entire armies can train together without leaving the ground. The benefits are striking: it saves lives by letting people make mistakes safely, saves enormous amounts of money, reduces environmental damage compared with live exercises, and sharpens the decisions that commanders make under pressure.

Yet the technology is not without cost. The huge price of building advanced systems, the "reality gap" that no simulation fully closes, cyber-security threats, and the ethical question of whether realistic virtual combat desensitises people are all genuine concerns that must be managed responsibly. The same headsets that prepare soldiers for war also help veterans heal from it — a powerful reminder that a tool's impact depends on how we choose to use it.

My view is that military simulation will keep growing because its advantages are simply too great to ignore — but its future depends on using it alongside real training and human judgement, not as a replacement for them. As artificial intelligence and immersive technology make these virtual worlds ever more lifelike, society will need clear rules to keep the technology safe, ethical and accessible. Handled wisely, military simulation can make defence forces both more capable and more humane: better prepared to protect people, and less likely to cause harm while they learn.

In one sentence Military simulation lets nations prepare for the worst day imaginable — while making it far less likely that anyone gets hurt in the process of getting ready.
